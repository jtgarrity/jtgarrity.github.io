Tagup helps Veolia avoid catastrophic transformer failures

Veolia, French multinational, provides a variety of municipal services, including electric generation. A Veolia cogeneration plant in the Greater Boston Area is working with Tagup to monitor their power transformers, helping to reduce unplanned downtime. Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA) sensors from Vaisala, a Finnish sensor manufacturer, were integrated with the Tagup platform. With Tagup, both the plant team and the sensor manufacturer can monitor transformer health anytime, anwhere. The continual monitoring can identify fast-forming faults, reducing unplanned downtime and lost revenue.

The Challenge

The steam and electricity generated by Veolia Kendall Cogeneration Station is critical to the city of Boston. Power transformers serve to get power reliably to and from the grid. If one of the plant's transformers were to fail, it could result in large amounts of downtime and lost revenue. Transformers are inspected twice a year, but there are faults that can occur within the inspection window. These fast-forming faults are difficult to detect, but can result in the catastrophic failure of a transformerâ€”an explosion.

The Solution

Using a Tagup-connected online DGA sensor, the plant team is able to monitor hydrogen levels in the transformer's oil, temperatures, and moisture content. This data is sent once per second to the Tagup cloud, where it is analyzed and relevant output presented to both the plant team and the sensor manufacturer. With Tagup, plant electricians and maintenance engineers can see transformer operating health and a single source of record for maintenance logs, inspection reports, and previous notifications and alarms. If the plant team has any questions on how best to interpret the data or an alarm, they can easily provide temporary access to a third-party transformer expert.

Key Results

By continually monitoring relevant health continously, and by applying Tagup's proprietary analytics methods, the Veolia team was able to reduce the probability of a failure occuring without advanced warning by nearly 70% (from an annual failure probability without warning of 0.35% down to 0.12%, based on IEEE C57.143-2012 and Hartford Steam Boiler claims data). This resulted in a series of benefits to the plant, principally deferred capital expenditures on transformer replacement and a reduction in lost revenue due to downtime. Annualy, this is worth $7k and $243k to the plant, respectively.
